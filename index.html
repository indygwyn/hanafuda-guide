Key pieces to know

Special cards 特別なカード

These cards drive the highest-value yaku. Learn to recognize them immediately on the field.

The five brights (hikari 光)

Crane + sun
January · Pine
White crane beneath a red sun. The most iconic hanafuda image.
Sakura curtain
March · Sakura blossom
A red curtain strung between trees for flower-viewing. Pairs with sake cup for hanami-zake.
Full moon
August · Susuki grass
Large yellow moon over pampas grass. Pairs with sake cup for tsukimizake.
Rainman
November · Willow
Ono no Tōfū in the rain. Reduces four brights from 8 to 7 pts. Doesn't count toward three brights at all.

Yellow paulownia with a Chinese phoenix. Completes the full five brights (goko).

Two butterflies among peonies. Completes ino-shika-cho. Appears in about 8% of rounds.

The sake cup — most flexible card

Sake cup
September · Chrysanthemum
Participates in tane yaku, hanami-zake (with Mar curtain), and tsukimizake (with Aug moon). Watch any opponent who holds it.

How to play

Rules ルール

Each turn has two phases. Match cards by month (same flower). Build yaku. Decide when to stop.

01
Setup
Shuffle 48 cards. Deal 8 to each player, 8 face-up to the center field, 24 remain as the draw pile. Redeal if 4 cards of the same month appear in the starting field.
02
Play from hand
Play one card from your hand. If it matches a field card by month (same flower), capture both. If two field cards match, choose a field card to capture. If no match, your card stays on the field.
03
Draw from pile
Flip the top draw card. Apply the same matching rule. If two field cards match, choose which one to capture — the other stays on the field.
04
Check for yaku
After both phases, check if you completed any yaku. If you have, you must declare immediately: koi-koi or shobu (stop). The round ends when a player stops, or the draw pile runs out.

The koi-koi decision

"Koi-koi!" — continue
Keep playing to build more yaku or higher scores. If you later call stop, your score doubles (×2). Risk: if your opponent completes a yaku and stops first, they score double and you get nothing.
"Shobu!" — stop
End the round now and bank your current points. Safe, but you may leave points on the table. If your opponent previously called koi-koi, your payout doubles when you stop.
Scoring multipliers: Add all yaku values first, then apply multipliers. Calling koi-koi and winning = ×2. Scoring 7+ pts in most variants = ×2. Both stack — maximum ×4.
Full game: 12 rounds, one per month. The dealer alternates each round. Highest cumulative total after all 12 rounds wins.
Playing to win

Strategy 戦略

Koi-koi rewards reading both your own hand and your opponent's captures.

Pursue efficient yaku
  • Poetry ribbons (akatan) and blue ribbons (aotan) both give 5 pts for 3 specific cards. If you start with 1–2 of a set, pursue it hard.
  • Ino-shika-cho gives 5 pts for 3 animals. Completes in about 8% of rounds — common enough to plan around.
  • Three brights (5 pts) requires 3 of the 5 bright cards. Strong if you start with 2 in hand.
When to call koi-koi
  • Call when you need only 1 more card for a yaku worth significantly more than your current score.
  • Call when the round has many turns left and your opponent has captured little.
  • Stop when your opponent already holds 2 brights, or has ribbons and animals accumulating.
  • Stop when 7+ pts gives you a strong multiplied payout regardless.
Deny your opponent
  • Track what your opponent captures. A player one bright away from three brights deserves aggressive denial — grab that bright even if it doesn't help your yaku.
  • The sake cup (September) participates in two drinking yaku. Watch any opponent who holds it.
  • When two field cards of the same month appear, your opponent can capture both with one play. Deny it by capturing one first.
The November (rainman) quirk
  • Rainman doesn't count toward three brights (sanko). It actively weakens your bright strategy.
  • Four brights with rainman = 7 pts (rainy four). Without rainman = 8 pts.
  • Starting with the rainman in hand changes which bright yaku remain achievable. Adjust plans accordingly.
Mix low-tier yaku
  • Most actual wins come from combining tane + tanzaku + kasu, not rare hands.
  • 24 of 48 cards are chaff. In a low-value round, whoever reaches 10 chaff first holds tempo.
  • Don't sacrifice naturally accumulating ribbons and animals while chasing a bright yaku that isn't materializing.
Risk calculus
  • Calling koi-koi when you hold 3 pts and your opponent scores means they get 6 pts and you get 0. The higher your current score, the more you risk.
  • Calling koi-koi repeatedly stacks multiplier potential — but also stacks risk. A 3-pt score doubled once = 6. If your opponent stops while you're chasing, you earned 0.
  • Early in a round, koi-koi costs little. Late in a round with few cards left, it costs a great deal.
